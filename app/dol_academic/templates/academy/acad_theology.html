A Structured Guide to Scholarly Theological Research

This document provides a comprehensive blueprint for developing, researching, and writing a formal academic thesis or paper in the field of theology.

Phase 1: Conception & Proposal Development

The goal of this phase is to move from a general interest to a specific, arguable, and academically significant research project.

1.1. Identify the General Field of Study

Begin by narrowing your focus to a specific domain.

Examples: Patristics (Early Church Fathers), Reformation History, Systematic Theology, Biblical Exegesis (e.g., Pauline Epistles), Political Theology, Feminist Theology, Philosophy of Religion.

My Field: [e.g., 20th Century Protestant Systematic Theology]

1.2. Establish the State of the Question (*Status Quaestionis*)

Conduct a preliminary literature review to understand the existing scholarly conversation. What has already been said? Where are the debates, tensions, or gaps?

Status Quaestionis Summary:

Scholar A ([Name of Scholar]) in their work ([Book/Article Title]) argues that [Scholar A's main point].

However, Scholar B ([Name of Scholar]) challenges this by positing that [Scholar B's counter-argument].

While this debate is well-established, a significant gap exists concerning [The specific gap or unexamined issue].

1.3. Formulate the Research Question

Turn the identified gap into a precise, guiding question.

Research Question: [e.g., How did Dietrich Bonhoeffer's understanding of 'costly grace' in "The Cost of Discipleship" directly inform his theory of state responsibility in the "Ethics" fragments?]

1.4. Craft the Thesis Statement

The thesis is the central, arguable claim that answers your research question. It is the single most important sentence of your entire project.

Thesis Statement: This study argues that [Your central, arguable claim]. It will demonstrate this by [briefly outlining the main steps of your argument, e.g., 'by first analyzing concept X, then tracing its development in text Y, and finally showing its impact on historical event Z.']

1.5. Define the Methodology and Scope

Clearly state the intellectual tools you will use and the boundaries of your research.

Methodology: This research will employ a [e.g., Historical-Critical Method combined with a Conceptual Analysis] to interpret the primary sources.

Scope & Delimitations: This study is limited to [e.g., the published and unpublished writings of Dietrich Bonhoeffer between 1937 and 1945]. It will not address [e.g., his earlier universalist tendencies or the reception of his work in post-war America].

Phase 2: Research & Source Engagement

This is the "data collection" phase. The goal is to master the primary texts and understand the secondary scholarship you plan to engage.

2.1. Assemble Primary & Secondary Sources

Primary Sources: The original texts or artifacts you are analyzing (e.g., The Bible, works of Aquinas, sermons, council documents).

Secondary Sources: The works of other scholars who have analyzed your topic or related primary sources.

Use a citation manager (e.g., Zotero, Mendeley) from the very beginning.

2.2. Critical Reading & Note-Taking

Read actively, not passively. Create a system for your notes (e.g., an annotated bibliography, a concept map).

Note-Taking Template for a Source:
  • Full Citation: [Chicago/Turabian style citation]
  • Author's Main Thesis: [What is the central argument of this book/article?]
  • Key Evidence Used: [What texts/data does the author use?]
  • Relevance to My Thesis: [How does this support, challenge, or add nuance to my argument?]
  • Key Quotes & Page Numbers: ["..." (p. ##)]

Phase 3: Analysis & Argument Construction

Move from collecting information to building your case. The deliverable of this phase is a detailed outline.

3.1. Synthesize and Categorize Notes

Organize your notes by theme, concept, or chronological development. Look for patterns, connections, and contradictions that support your thesis.

3.2. Develop a Detailed Outline

This is the architectural plan for your paper. Each point should represent a paragraph or section that contributes to proving your main thesis.

Thesis Outline Structure:
  1. Introduction
    • Hook/Opening
    • Status Quaestionis (The scholarly context and gap)
    • Thesis Statement
    • Methodology and Roadmap (Outline of chapters)
  2. Chapter 1: [e.g., Historical and Theological Context]
  3. Chapter 2: [e.g., Close Reading of Primary Source A]
  4. Chapter 3: [e.g., Conceptual Analysis of 'Concept X']
  5. Chapter 4: [e.g., Synthesis and Implications]
  6. Conclusion
    • Restatement of Thesis and Summary of Argument
    • Statement of Contribution (The "So what?")
    • Limitations and Avenues for Future Research

Phase 4: Drafting & Finalization

Translate your outline into prose. Writing is a process of discovery; expect your ideas to become clearer as you write.

4.1. Write the First Draft

Focus on getting your argument down on paper. Follow your outline but do not worry about perfect prose or grammar. This is the "get it done" draft.

4.2. Refine and Edit

Revise in multiple passes, focusing on one thing at a time:

  1. Argument & Structure: Is the logic sound? Does each chapter support the main thesis? Is the flow clear?
  2. Clarity & Style: Is the prose clear, concise, and academic in tone?
  3. Proofreading: Check for grammar, spelling, and punctuation errors. Read it aloud.

4.3. Finalize Formatting, Citations, and Bibliography

Ensure every claim is properly cited according to your required style guide (typically Chicago/Turabian in theology). Check that your bibliography is complete and perfectly formatted.

4.4. The Final Checklist

  • [ ] Title Page is correctly formatted.
  • [ ] Table of Contents is accurate.
  • [ ] Introduction contains a clear thesis statement.
  • [ ] Each paragraph has a clear topic sentence.
  • [ ] All sources are cited correctly in footnotes/endnotes.
  • [ ] Bibliography matches all cited sources.
  • [ ] Page numbers are correct.
  • [ ] The document has been spell-checked and proofread.
